# MidAmerica_V2 Visual Standard

## Purpose
This document defines the minimum acceptable standard for `MidAmerica_V2`.

The site is not allowed to be merely tasteful, clean, or premium-adjacent.
It needs to feel expensive, composed, and visually authored enough to plausibly attract wealthy aircraft owners, family offices, and serious flight departments.

If a section feels like a nice template, a polished wireframe, or a collection of well-spaced boxes, it fails.

## Non-Negotiables

### 1. No Pills
- No bloated rounded capsules.
- No oversized bubble cards.
- No soft gummy UI.
- Corners should feel architectural, sharp, and intentional.
- If a component feels like a pill, it is wrong even if the copy is good.

### 2. No Clipping, Cropping, or Awkward Wraps
- No text should be visually cut off.
- No right rails should squeeze copy into ugly narrow columns.
- No heading should wrap in a way that feels accidental or crippled.
- No section background should visually collide with the heading above it.
- If something looks even slightly broken in a screenshot, it fails.

### 3. Every Page Must Have a Strong Composition
- A page cannot feel like stacked rectangles.
- Each hero needs a deliberate focal point, not just text on one side and image on the other.
- Each major section should read as one composition, not many separate cards competing for attention.
- The eye should know what matters first, second, and third.

### 4. Luxury Means Restraint Plus Authority
- “Expensive” does not mean generic black-and-gold or more decoration.
- It means fewer, stronger moves.
- Typography must feel decisive.
- Image framing must feel curated.
- Contrast must feel controlled.
- Layout rhythm must feel authored, not auto-generated.

### 5. White Space Must Feel Intentional, Not Empty
- Open space is only acceptable if it increases drama or focus.
- If space feels like missing content or loose layout construction, it fails.
- Large quiet areas need an image, a directional line, a strong typographic move, or a deliberate pause.

### 6. Dark Sections Must Earn Their Presence
- Dark bands cannot exist just to look premium.
- They must hold the composition together and make the page feel more commanding.
- If a dark slab cuts into content awkwardly, clips a heading, or feels pasted on, it fails.

### 7. Images Must Do Real Work
- Photography needs to carry mood, trust, and aspiration.
- Images cannot feel like placeholders that happen to be high quality.
- The crop, position, and scale need to feel chosen.
- If the photo feels boxed in, under-scaled, or decorative, it fails.

### 8. Wealthy Clients Notice Cheap Layout Decisions
- Uneven spacing, clipped text, weak hierarchy, accidental overlaps, and timid layout choices all read as cheap.
- “Close enough” is not acceptable.
- If something feels unresolved in a screenshot, assume the user will see it immediately.

### 9. Hero Sections Must Win Immediately
- The first screen should feel unmistakably premium within seconds.
- It should have a commanding headline, a believable image stage, and a clean action path.
- No hero should feel generic, over-boxed, or visually confused.

### 10. Internal Pages Cannot Drop Below the Homepage
- Services, Capabilities, Ownership, About, and Contact must feel equally authored.
- Internal pages cannot become more templated, more cramped, or more repetitive than the home page.
- Every page needs one memorable section, not just competent structure.

## Failure Patterns To Reject Immediately
- Pill-shaped anything.
- Rounded floating cards that feel soft or consumer-app-like.
- Narrow sidebar cards that force ugly line breaks.
- Dark background blocks that cut through content awkwardly.
- Headings that feel too tall, too cramped, or visually stranded.
- Sections that read like “headline plus card grid” with no stronger visual idea.
- Large dead zones of empty beige background.
- Components that look correct in code but broken in screenshots.
- Any composition that looks like it came from a premium SaaS template rather than elite aviation branding.

## Audit Questions
For every page, ask:

1. Would a millionaire think this feels expensive or just polished?
2. Would a chief pilot think this feels disciplined or just stylish?
3. Is anything clipped, cramped, oddly wrapped, or visually colliding?
4. Is the hierarchy obvious in three seconds?
5. Is there at least one composition on the page that feels memorable?
6. Does any component still look like a pill, rounded card, or template artifact?
7. Does the photography feel integrated or merely placed?
8. Would this page embarrass the brand if it were the first screenshot someone saw?

If any answer is uncomfortable, the page is not done.
